The HiPEAC Vision provides a long-term (10-year +) view of trends in computing systems. For researchers, it offers valuable guidance to the topics which will be most important in the future, as well as showing how computing research can impact society.

The following areas are key for the development of computing systems over the next few years.

Vision: a convergence of key technologies from the web, cyber-physical systems (CPS), the cloud, the internet of things (IoT), digital twins, and artificial intelligence (AI) into a coherent, federated ecosystem. This ecosystem emphasizes spatial computing, (generative) AI at the edge, dynamic web integration, user-centric models and intelligent, efficient and trustable orchestration of distributed services. The NCP will be implemented via non-proprietary, vertical integrations in a series of compelling use cases.

Key drivers:

  • Data and interconnected machines: the NCP will have to integrate the ‘web of machines’ with the ‘web of humans’.
  • Large-scale, safety-critical systems and digital twins: bringing new requirements to the web, including safety, real time and location.
  • The spatial web: going ‘into’ the computing continuum.
  • Everything as a service: applications for users will be composed by tailored coordination of services (including digital twins) to be run where it is most efficient.
  • Smart orchestrators to orchestrate services according to the user’s requirements, and to protect and guide users in a trustable way. These will use AI for smooth, natural communication with humans.

Key areas of research to enable the NCP:

  • 4D-aware implementation technologies to support spatial and time-aware computing.
    • Merging HTTP-based REST-like protocols with spatial web protocols.
    • Augmenting all such protocols so that they can guarantee timely delivery, for any granularity of time.
  • Augmenting application programming interfaces (APIs) with contract-based specifications that can allow compositions (and interoperability) based on assume / guarantee pairings with bi-authentication.
    • Improving WebAssembly-type technology to allow hosting and efficient execution of sandboxed in-transit computations.
    • Allowing computations to move after travelling users or associated objects, seeking the optimal balance of latency, cybersecurity, privacy , data freshness, and energy requirements.
  • Connected, intelligent, cyber-physical systems that ensure physical safety, respect privacy, and defend against cyberattacks.
  • Swarm computing supporting opportunistic dynamic and mobile aggregations of compute nodes within variable-sized logical regions.
  • AI-powered, edge-based, trustworthy orchestrators that dynamically and opportunistically assemble APIs into ad hoc, private, service compositions.
  • Using digital twins to model full systems and engineer optimizations holistically

Vision: the EU as a strong player in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly for widespread use at the edge.

Key research areas:

  • Core technologies for multimodal AI development linked to key application areas for growth.
  • Methodologies and datasets to develop and finetune open-source foundational models to suit specific regional needs.
  • Local solutions and specialized accelerators for the integration of large language models (LLMs) in smart devices at the edge.
  • Using AI in hardware and software development.
  • ‘Correctness by construction’ in AI models, including methods that automatically verify the correctness of AI-generated outputs.
  • Developing and applying AI in an ethical way.
  • Explainable AI.
  • Sovereign, secured AI.

Vision: Innovative computing technologies which offer significant energy efficiency gains and more sustainable means of production. A thriving, sovereign European chiplet ecosystem, promoting innovation in this field.

Key research areas:

  • Emerging technologies such as photonics, spintronics, stacked nanosheet field-effect transistors (FETs), new memory technologies, organic electronics and flexible electronics.
  • Interdisciplinary research on new hardware paradigms, including quantum computing, neuromorphic computing, new materials, and information theory, in parallel with system architects and the software community to ensure that the solutions created are efficient and usable.
  • Innovative architectures including in-memory computing, 3D stacking, and adaptive precision.
  • New intellectual property in the value chain to help expand an ecosystem of interoperable chiplets in Europe.
  • Solutions that promote sustainability in hardware and software development, addressing issues such as energy consumption, CO2 emissions, and material usage.
  • Hardware solutions that promote trustability, privacy and cybersecurity

Vision: Cybersecurity as a major upfront concern in every computing system, all the more with a highly interconnected NCP. An NCP built from the outset to prevent rogue or fake services and malicious use as far as possible, with security, authentication and verification present at every level.

Key research areas:

  • Cybersecurity and privacy-protecting tools and techniques fit for an increasingly connected world, including trusted computing base, quantitative security metrics, automated cybersecurity analyses and cyber-defence.
  • Cybersecurity of the software supply chain, both at the code level, with vulnerability analyses and repair techniques, and at the toolchain level, with secured package managers and distribution tools.
  • Cryptography, including fully homomorphic encryption, post-quantum cryptography.
  • AI-based techniques, including large language models (LLMs), for automated cybersecurity analysis and defence
  • Cybersecurity of LLMs and prevention of malicious use
  • Cybersecurity of distributed ledgers
  • Methodologies to ensure trustworthiness throughout the integrated-hardware circuits supply chain

Vision: Sustainability lifecycle assessments for all new computing systems, encompassing production, materials, shipping, usage, end-of-life processing, etc. Devices that are used for as long as possible, that are easy to repair and recycle, and that are built using recycled components.

Key research areas:

  • Validated lifecycle models and sustainability modelling tools
  • Circular concepts and design frameworks for full lifecycle management
  • Modular systems that are easy to put together, take apart and repair.
  • Repairability benchmarking
  • Technologies that use fewer raw materials
  • Component recycling
