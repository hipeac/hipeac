The HiPEAC Vision provides a long-term (10-year +) view of trends in computing systems. For researchers, it offers valuable guidance to the topics which will be most important in the future, as well as showing how computing research can impact society.

The following areas are key for the development of computing systems over the next few years.

Vision: A future internet of things (IoT) including cyber-physical systems, accessed via the spatial web, which is offered as an ‘as-a-service’ model on the user’s device of choice.

Requirements:

  • Safety: The next web will have to respect the constraints of the physical world. For example, real time and safety will have to be incorporated into the computation.
  • Interoperability: The compute continuum will need to provide seamless distribution of computations where they make most sense.

The following enabling technologies will be key:

  • Artificial intelligence (AI), both central and at the edge, to enable decision making and to facilitate better integration between the physical and digital worlds
  • The tactile internet for interaction
  • Swarm intelligence for collaboration
  • Orchestration across the compute continuum

Vision: Enhanced EU presence in AI technologies and systems. AI applications which deliver real social and economic value in Europe, especially in embedded AI.

Requirements:

  • Trust: methods to identify bias and misbehaviour.
  • Frugality: hardware, software and algorithms for more energy-efficient (while still high-performance) AI, suitable for edge devices.

Key research areas:

  • Natural language processing
  • Pruning, quantization, efficient algorithms
  • Algorithmic approaches combined with deep neural networks
  • Energy-efficient AI hardware
  • AI as a support for software and hardware development
  • AI ethics

Vision: Innovative computing technologies which offer significant energy efficiency gains and more sustainable means of production, and which are fully integrated into hybrid systems including legacy technologies. Building upon European strengths in the fundamental fields of computing (information theory, complexity theory, computer architecture, algorithm design, programming paradigms).

Requirements:

  • Integration: In addition to the core technology, emerging paradigms need to be worked into existing systems, complementing rather than replacing traditional CMOS-based designs.
  • Practicality: Research should be coupled to practical applications.

Key research areas:

  • Quantum computing
  • Neuromorphic computing
  • Spintronics
  • Photonics
  • In-memory computing
  • Flexible electronics
  • System architecture and software stack for emerging computing paradigms

Vision: Reinforced cyber defences for critical infrastructure and supply chains. Tools and methods to make security (and privacy) a first-class citizen during the development of new ICT systems, identify and mitigate vulnerabilities in existing ICT systems. EU-based audit and certification of ICT systems with liability for sub-par security. EU-designed and EU-audited quantum-resistant encryption schemes, hardware and software.

Key research areas:

  • Cybersecurity tools and techniques, including automated cyber defence, quantitative security metrics, code analysis, refactoring tools, randomization, trusted computing base
  • Cryptography, including fully homomorphic encryption, post-quantum cryptography
  • Blockchain

Vision: Europe (re)gaining control over essential digital infrastructure. Well-funded competence centres attracting top talent to keep Europe at the forefront of technology development. Strategic use of open source to support innovation and collaboration and to expand European technology ecosystems without fear of export restrictions.

Key research areas:

  • Prototypes for open-source solutions across the computing stack
  • Made-in-EU digital essentials / failsafes, e.g. alternatives to proprietary or control-locked offerings such as Office and Android
  • International enclave management
  • Digital solutions for challenges such as the ageing population and sustainability

Vision: Computing systems designed on the basis of a full lifecycle assessment to ensure overall optimization for sustainability. Long-lasting devices that are easy to repair and recycle, and that are built using recycled components. Where appropriate, digital alternatives to physical goods and services. Europe as a leader in sustainable electronics and in digital solutions that promote a more sustainable way of life and/or adaptations to climate change (e.g. renewable power distribution, precision agriculture, extreme weather forecasting, etc.).

Key research areas:

  • Reducing the embodied footprint of computing systems
  • Sustainability modelling tools
  • Circular concepts and design frameworks for full lifecycle management
  • Modular systems that are easy to put together and take apart
  • Technologies that use fewer raw materials
  • Component recycling
  • Repairability benchmarking
